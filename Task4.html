Task 4

Snell’s law from Fermat: least-time path across a boundary between two media (speeds c₁ and c₂)

Overview

For points A above and B below a horizontal boundary, compute the travel time t(x) for a ray that crosses the interface at horizontal position x when wave speeds are c₁ (upper medium) and c₂ (lower medium). Plot t versus x, locate the minimum, and use it to verify Snell’s law.

Key Theory

Fermat’s principle states that light (or any wavefront) follows a path that makes the travel time stationary. With constant speeds c₁ and c₂ in two uniform media, the time for a path A→S→B that touches the interface y = 0 at S = (x, 0) is t(x) = d₁/c₁ + d₂/c₂, where d₁ = √(x² + y₁²) and d₂ = √((L − x)² + y₂²). Minimizing t(x) with respect to x gives sinθ/c₁ = sinφ/c₂, which is Snell’s law in speed form. For light, since n ∝ 1/c, this is equivalent to n₁ sinθ = n₂ sinφ.

Here θ is the angle of incidence in the upper medium and φ is the angle of refraction in the lower medium, both measured from the normal. If c₂ < c₁ (slower lower medium, higher refractive index), the ray bends towards the normal (φ < θ). The t(x) curve is convex with a single minimum at the physically realized crossing point.
