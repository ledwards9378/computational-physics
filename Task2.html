Task 2

Verify the thin lens equation and determine the focal length from a 1/v vs 1/u linear fit

Overview

Measure object distance u and image distance v for a converging (biconvex) lens, plot 1/v against 1/u, fit a straight line, and extract the focal length f from the intercept. Report f in centimetres with a brief comment on the model’s validity.

Key Theory

A thin, symmetric converging lens bends paraxial rays so that rays parallel to the optical axis meet at the focal point a distance f from the lens. For real images and objects, the thin lens equation relates object distance u and image distance v (measured from the lens along the optic axis): 1/u + 1/v = 1/f.

Sign convention used here: u and v are taken as positive for a real object and a real (inverted) image on opposite sides of the lens. For a converging lens, f > 0. With all distances in centimetres, 1/u and 1/v have units of 1/cm and 1/f is also in 1/cm.

Linearisation for analysis: rearrange to a straight-line form in the variables x = 1/u and y = 1/v, y = −x + 1/f. The theoretical slope is −1 and the intercept on the y-axis is 1/f. Therefore, fitting a line y = mx + c to your 1/v vs 1/u data gives f = 1/c. Deviations of m from −1 and scatter about the line indicate experimental/systematic errors (alignment, non-paraxial rays, finite lens thickness, measurement precision).

Example (from the provided dataset): a best-fit line yields approximately m ≈ −1.04 and c ≈ 0.0669 (1/cm), giving f ≈ 1/c ≈ 15.0 cm and a strong linear correlation (R² ≈ 0.998). These figures are typical for a school lab biconvex lens and support the thin lens model within experimental uncertainty.
