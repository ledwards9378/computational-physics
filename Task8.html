Task 8

Concave spherical mirror: interactive real-image formation with pixel coordinate mapping

Overview

Load an object and move it near a concave spherical mirror of radius R. The program warps the image using a coordinate transform that follows the mirror geometry and shows the real inverted image that can appear “floating” in front of the mirror. It overlays a paraxial prediction (mirror equation) and draws principal rays for intuition.

Key Theory

For a spherical concave mirror of radius R (vertex at x = −R, center at (0,0)), the focal length is f = R/2. Using the standard paraxial mirror relation with distances measured from the vertex along the optic axis, 1/u + 1/v = 1/f, where u is the object distance (to the right of the vertex) and v is the image distance. When u > f, a real inverted image forms at x = −R + v; magnification is m = −v/u.

Beyond the small‑angle approximation, each pixel can be mapped with a geometry‑based transform that uses the surface normal and reflection law (equivalent to the equations shown in the brief). This produces a realistic warped image on the mirror. Ray tracing (a central ray via the mirror’s center and a ray parallel to the axis reflecting through the focus) is drawn to show how the real image position is consistent with both the transform and the paraxial prediction.
