Task 11a

Rainbow elevation ε vs incidence θ — primary and secondary bows

Overview

Plot the deflection (elevation) angle ε of light emerging from a spherical water drop as a function of the incident angle θ. The curves are coloured by wavelength using a dispersion model for water, so the rainbow structure is visible directly on the plot.

Key Theory

Let r be the refraction angle inside the drop, given by Snell’s law r = sin⁻¹(sin θ / n). With n the refractive index of water (slightly wavelength‑dependent), the deflection ε for the two main rainbow orders is:

  • Primary (one internal reflection): ε(θ) = 4r − 2θ = 4 sin⁻¹(sin θ / n) − 2θ
  • Secondary (two internal reflections): ε(θ) = π − 6r + 2θ = π − 6 sin⁻¹(sin θ / n) + 2θ

The brightest part of each bow occurs near the stationary point dε/dθ = 0. Solving gives closed‑form incident angles:

  • Primary: θ⋆ = sin⁻¹(√((4 − n²)/3))
  • Secondary: θ⋆ = sin⁻¹(√((9 − n²)/8))

For n ≈ 1.33 (green light in water), the primary bow emerges near ε ≈ 41–43° and the secondary near ε ≈ 50–53°. The script samples visible wavelengths, maps each to n(λ), and draws the families ε(θ) in their corresponding colours with the turning‑point levels indicated.
